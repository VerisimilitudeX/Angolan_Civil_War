Angolan Civil War (1975–2002)

The Angolan Civil War was a major armed conflict emerging soon after Angola became independent from Portugal in 1975. It involved multiple factions and outside influences, ultimately shaping modern Angola's political and social landscape.

Overview

The Angolan Civil War was rooted in the power struggle between different liberation movements that had fought Portuguese colonial rule. Over the decades, it drew involvement from international actors such as the Soviet Union, Cuba, the United States, and South Africa. The conflict officially concluded in 2002 after decades of turmoil, leaving Angola with significant reconstruction challenges.

Timeline of Key Events

1975: Outbreak of the Civil War

November 1975

Angola declares independence from Portugal. Conflict quickly ignites between the MPLA, UNITA, and FNLA.

With the sudden collapse of Portuguese rule, different political factions vie for control. The MPLA (People's Movement for the Liberation of Angola) eventually takes Luanda, while UNITA and FNLA form opposition in the south and north.

1976: MPLA Gains International Recognition

1976

The MPLA receives recognition by many countries and the UN, consolidating power.

Soviet and Cuban support helps the MPLA maintain a stronger military advantage. The US and South Africa provide varying levels of aid to UNITA.

1991: Bicesse Accords

May 1991

The Angolan government and UNITA sign the Bicesse Accords, aiming for a multi-party democracy and ceasefire.

Despite the accords, tensions remain high, and disarmament efforts stall. A presidential election is held in 1992, but renewed violence breaks out when UNITA disputes the results.

1994: Lusaka Protocol

November 1994

Another attempt at peace through the Lusaka Protocol between the MPLA-led government and UNITA.

The agreement reaffirms prior accords and calls for integration of UNITA forces into the national army. However, full compliance remains elusive and fighting continues in some regions.

2002: End of the War

April 2002

Following the death of UNITA leader Jonas Savimbi, a ceasefire is reached and the war officially ends.

This marks the formal conclusion of one of Africa’s longest conflicts. Reconstruction and reconciliation efforts begin, though challenges remain in rebuilding infrastructure and addressing long-standing grievances.
